# Origin

For reasons I don't really understand, I was drawn to expressing my Cystic Fibrosis through art. Some people may think of "disease and art" as a somber subject. Spanish Flu period pieces, 17th century plauge doctor, and the like. But it never really felt like that to me.

See, I grew up in the world of [65 roses](https://www.cff.org/about-us/65-roses-story). It's the kind of spirit where we give our disease a fun, quirky name. Where my Walkathon team has more clothing designs than most professional sports teams. Where being hospitalized is simply an opportunity to binge Nintendo 64. Where we know the importance of raising money for the CF Foundation (and know that the best strategy is to ensure that all guests are properly inebriated). Where CF more aptly stands for "Cure Found".

With that upbringing, my artistic journey began as an 18-year old. As a biology-curious high-schooler, I happened to get my hands on a copy of the original genetic test with my CF results from my infancy. It's where I first became aware of the names of my two CF genes: deltaF508 and G551D. I was learning about things like mitosis and transcription these days, and went down some rabbit holes here to learn more.

*Side note: two years later, when trials for the investigational drug ivacaftor (now a [blockbuster drug](https://news.vrtx.com/news-releases/news-release-details/fda-approves-kalydecotm-ivacaftor-first-medicine-treat) named Kalydeco) started recruiting patients with the rare G551D mutation, my personal genetic knowledge allowed me to jump at the opportunity immediately.*

Like any good basketball player, I decided that it would be cool to get these gene names tattooed on me. A couple key facts were not unbeknownst to me as the needle met my skin in a random tattoo shop outside of Pittsburgh. My tatttoo was taking place 18 years after the [identification of the CFTR gene](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.2475911) led by Francis Collins, Lap-Chee Tsui, and John Riordan in 1989. The timing also corresponded to the 18-year life expectancy often cited in CF articles written in the 90's. I guess 18 is something of an important number. If I ever become a dark wizard, I'll be sure to create 18 Horcruxes.

It was only fitting then, that after another 18 years from that moment, it was time to embark on the next phase of the journey, this time naturally shifting from tattoo guns to programming and robots.
